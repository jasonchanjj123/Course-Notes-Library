Chapter Summary

Core concepts, definitions, and key mechanics from the lecture slides.

What Are Money Markets?

Money markets are financial markets that trade debt securities or instruments with maturities of less than one year. They arise because the immediate cash needs of individuals, corporations, and governments do not always coincide with their cash receipts. Once issued, money market instruments trade in active secondary markets, giving investors the ability to convert holdings to cash quickly.

🔑

Three Universal Characteristics

All money market instruments share: (1) generally sold in large denominations ($1M–$10M); (2) low default risk; (3) original maturity of one year or less.

Yield Conventions

Money market yields are quoted in two different ways, and understanding the distinction is critical for comparing instruments.

Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY)
P₀ = Pf / (1 + i_bey × n/365)
Uses a 365-day year. Represents the annualized return on securities priced below face value.
Discount Yield (Discount Basis)
P₀ = Pf / (1 + i_d × n/360)
Uses a 360-day year. Used for T-Bills and commercial paper. Tends to understate true return vs. BEY.
ℹ️

Why Two Conventions?

The discount yield (360-day) is a market convention inherited from pre-computer banking. The BEY (365-day) is considered more accurate for comparing with bond returns. The BEY is always slightly higher than the discount yield for the same security.

Treasury Bills (T-Bills)

Treasury Bills are short-term U.S. government obligations sold to fund government budget deficits and refinance maturing debt. They are also the primary tool the Federal Reserve uses to implement monetary policy via open market operations.

  • Original maturities: 4, 13, 26, and 52 weeks
  • Issued in multiples of $100
  • Virtually default-risk free; minimal interest rate and liquidity risk
  • Sold at a discount from face value; return comes from the difference between purchase price and face value at maturity

T-Bill Auction Process

The U.S. Treasury sells new T-Bills through a formal auction. Bids may be competitive — specifying the desired amount and discount yield, typically used by large investors and government securities dealers — or noncompetitive, limited to $5M per bidder, specifying only the desired face value. Noncompetitive bidders accept whatever yield clears the auction.

Secondary Market for T-Bills

The secondary market for T-Bills is the largest of any U.S. money market security. 24 primary government securities dealers are designated to purchase most competitively bid T-Bills and maintain an active secondary market. Transactions between primary dealers are conducted over the Federal Reserve's wire transfer service — Fedwire. Around 500 smaller dealers also trade in this market.

Federal Funds

Federal funds (fed funds) are short-term funds transferred between financial institutions, usually overnight. Commercial banks trade excess reserves held at their local Federal Reserve Bank. The fed funds rate is determined by supply and demand for these reserves, as well as the Federal Reserve's FOMC operations.

⚠️

Counterparty Risk in Fed Funds

The primary risk of the interbank lending system is that the borrowing bank does not pledge collateral. Loans are typically oral agreements between correspondent banks for millions of dollars — entirely unsecured.

Repurchase Agreements (Repos)

A repurchase agreement (repo or RP) involves the sale of securities by one party to another with a promise to repurchase them at a specified price on a specified date. Repos are collateralized — the seller holds securities to back the transaction — and a haircut is applied, meaning the loan is slightly smaller than the market value of pledged collateral.

  • Overnight repos: one-day maturity
  • Term repos: longer maturities
  • Reverse repo: the purchase side — the party buying the securities with a promise to sell them back

Commercial Paper

Commercial paper (CP) is an unsecured short-term promissory note issued by a company to raise short-term cash, commonly for working capital. It is one of the largest money market instruments by dollar volume (~$1.1 trillion outstanding as of Dec 2019). Companies with strong credit ratings can borrow more cheaply via CP than bank loans.

  • Denominations: $100,000; $250,000; $500,000; $1M
  • Maturities: 1–270 days (most common: 20–45 days)
  • Quoted on a discount basis (360-day year)
  • Sold directly via issuer's sales force or indirectly through brokers/dealers (more expensive)
🚨

Asset-Backed Commercial Paper (ABCP) & the 2008 Crisis

In July 2007, $1.19T of $2.16T total CP outstanding was ABCP — collateralized by financial assets, largely mortgage-backed securities. When subprime mortgages deteriorated, billions in ABCP were tainted, and outstanding CP levels continued to fall even after markets stabilized.

Negotiable Certificates of Deposit (CDs)

A negotiable CD is a bank-issued, fixed-maturity, interest-bearing time deposit that is negotiable — it can be traded in secondary markets any number of times. It is a bearer instrument, meaning whoever holds it at maturity receives principal and interest.

  • Denominations: $100,000–$10M (most common: $1M)
  • Common maturities: 1, 2, 3, 6, and 12 months
  • Interest rates quoted using a 360-day year
  • Large, well-known banks offer slightly lower rates than smaller banks

Banker's Acceptances (BAs)

A banker's acceptance is a time draft payable to a seller of goods, with payment guaranteed by a bank. BAs often arise from international trade transactions. They are payable to the bearer at maturity and trade in secondary markets in round lots of $100,000 and $500,000. Since June 2002, BA levels are included in commercial paper statistics.

Feynman Explanation
Discount Yield vs. Bond Equivalent Yield
▾

The Simple Version

Imagine you buy a $1,000 T-Bill for $980. After 90 days, you get back $1,000 — earning $20. But how do you express that as a yearly rate? That's where the two conventions differ.

Discount Yield: The "Old School" Way

The discount yield says: "Your $20 profit, divided by the $1,000 face value (not what you paid!), scaled to a 360-day year." This gives a smaller number than reality because you divide by the larger face value and use only 360 days.

Bond Equivalent Yield: The "Accurate" Way

The BEY says: "Your $20 profit, divided by the $980 you actually paid, scaled to a 365-day year." Since you divide by a smaller number (your actual investment) and use 365 days, BEY is always higher than the discount yield for the same instrument.

Why It Matters

When comparing a T-Bill to a corporate bond, you must convert the T-Bill's discount yield to BEY — otherwise you're comparing apples to oranges. The BEY is the apples-to-apples number.

Feynman Explanation
How the T-Bill Auction Works
▾

The Setup

The U.S. Treasury needs cash and decides to sell $10 billion of 13-week T-Bills. It announces the auction, and investors line up to buy.

Two Kinds of Bidders

Competitive bidders (big banks, hedge funds) say: "I'll buy $500 million — but only if the yield is at least 4.95%." They're naming their price. If the auction clears at 4.90%, their bid might not be filled. Noncompetitive bidders (small investors) say: "I want $50,000 worth — I'll take whatever yield the auction produces." They're guaranteed to get their allocation, limited to $5 million per person.

How It Clears

The Treasury sorts all competitive bids from lowest yield (highest price) to highest yield (lowest price). It accepts bids starting from the lowest yield until it has raised the $10 billion it needs. The highest yield accepted is the "stop-out rate" — everyone gets that yield, whether they bid higher or lower.

After the Auction

Primary dealers buy most of the bills, then sell them to other investors in the secondary market — creating the most liquid money market in the world.

Feynman Explanation
Repurchase Agreements (Repos) Explained
▾

Think of It as a Collateralized Loan

A repo is essentially a short-term loan dressed up as a sale-and-repurchase. Here's the mechanic: Bank A needs cash overnight. It sells $100M of T-Bills to Investor B for $99.9M, and simultaneously signs a contract saying "I'll buy them back tomorrow for $100M."

Who Gets What

Investor B gets securities (collateral) plus a small return ($0.1M) overnight. Bank A gets the $99.9M cash it needs. The "haircut" — the difference between market value of collateral ($100M) and the loan amount ($99.9M) — protects Investor B if Bank A defaults.

The Reverse Repo

The same transaction looks like a "reverse repo" from Investor B's perspective: they're buying securities with a promise to sell them back. One party's repo is always the other party's reverse repo.

Why It Matters Systemically

Repos are how large financial institutions manage daily cash balances. The repo market is enormous — trillions of dollars daily — and its stability is crucial to the functioning of bond markets.

Feynman Explanation
Federal Funds & the Overnight Market
▾

The Reserve Requirement Problem

Every commercial bank must hold a certain level of reserves at the Fed. At the end of each day, some banks find they have more reserves than required; others find they have too few.

The Solution: Trading Reserves Overnight

A bank with excess reserves will call a "correspondent bank" — a bank it has a relationship with — and offer to lend those reserves overnight. The borrowing bank pays the fed funds rate for the use of the money, then returns the reserves the next morning.

What Makes It Unique

These loans are typically completely unsecured — no collateral, just a phone call or electronic instruction. Amounts are often in the millions. The trust between correspondent banks is the only guarantee. This is why the fed funds market is considered a measure of financial system confidence.

The Fed's Role

The Federal Reserve influences the fed funds rate by adding or draining reserves from the system through open market operations. When the Fed buys T-Bills, it adds reserves (pushing the rate down). When it sells T-Bills, it drains reserves (pushing the rate up).

💥
Feynman Explanation
ABCP and Why It Collapsed in 2007–2008
▾

The Idea Behind ABCP

Regular commercial paper is just an IOU from a company. Asset-backed commercial paper adds a twist: a special vehicle issues the paper, and the collateral backing it is a pool of other financial assets — theoretically reducing risk by diversification.

The Mid-2000s Explosion

By mid-2007, ABCP had grown to $1.19 trillion of the $2.16 trillion CP market. The collateral backing this paper was heavily concentrated in mortgage-backed securities, many tied to subprime home loans.

The Collapse

When U.S. housing prices started falling in 2006–2007, those subprime mortgages began defaulting. Suddenly nobody knew what ABCP was really worth — the assets backing it were toxic. Investors refused to roll over (renew) their ABCP holdings. The market froze almost overnight.

The Lesson

Money market instruments are supposed to be boring — short-term, low-risk, liquid. ABCP violated that promise by stuffing long-duration, high-risk, illiquid assets into a short-term wrapper. The mismatch between the instrument's apparent safety and the actual risk of its collateral was catastrophic.

Practice Exercises

Tier 1 · Recall
Definitions, facts, and basic identification
Exercise 1
List three characteristics shared by all money market instruments.
(1) Generally sold in large denominations ($1M–$10M); (2) low default risk — low risk of late or nonpayment of principal/interest; (3) original maturity of one year or less.
Exercise 2
What are the four original maturities available for U.S. Treasury Bills?
4 weeks, 13 weeks (3 months), 26 weeks (6 months), and 52 weeks (1 year).
Exercise 3
What is a repurchase agreement (repo)? Name the two parties and their roles.
A repo is an agreement where one party (the seller/borrower) sells securities to another party (the buyer/lender) with a promise to repurchase them at a specified price on a specified future date. The seller obtains short-term financing; the buyer earns a short-term return on collateralized funds.
Exercise 4
What is commercial paper, and what type of company can issue it?
Commercial paper is an unsecured short-term promissory note issued by companies to raise short-term cash (typically for working capital). Only companies with strong credit ratings typically issue it, as it is unsecured — investors rely on the issuer's creditworthiness.
Exercise 5
What is the difference between overnight repos and term repos?
Overnight repos have a one-day maturity — the securities are repurchased the next day. Term repos have longer maturities, ranging from several days to months, used when the borrower needs funding for an extended period.
Tier 2 · Application
Calculations and concept application
Exercise 6
A 90-day T-Bill with a $10,000 face value is purchased at a discount yield of 4.8%. Calculate the purchase price P₀. (Use the discount yield formula with a 360-day year.)
P₀ = Pf / (1 + i_d × n/360) = $10,000 / (1 + 0.048 × 90/360) = $10,000 / (1 + 0.012) = $10,000 / 1.012 ≈ $9,881.42. The investor pays approximately $9,881.42 and receives $10,000 at maturity.
Exercise 7
Using the same T-Bill from Exercise 6 (purchase price $9,881.42, face value $10,000, 90-day maturity), calculate the Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY). Is it higher or lower than the 4.8% discount yield? Why?
BEY: P₀ = Pf / (1 + i_bey × n/365) → $9,881.42 = $10,000 / (1 + i_bey × 90/365). Rearranging: (1 + i_bey × 0.2466) = $10,000 / $9,881.42 = 1.01201. So i_bey × 0.2466 = 0.01201, i_bey ≈ 4.87%. The BEY (≈4.87%) is higher than the discount yield (4.8%) because: (1) BEY divides by the actual purchase price (smaller denominator) rather than face value, and (2) BEY scales to 365 days rather than 360.
Exercise 8
A company issues 30-day commercial paper with a face value of $1,000,000 at a discount yield of 5.2%. What is the issue price? What is the dollar return to the investor?
P₀ = $1,000,000 / (1 + 0.052 × 30/360) = $1,000,000 / (1 + 0.004333) = $1,000,000 / 1.004333 ≈ $995,683. Dollar return = $1,000,000 − $995,683 = $4,317. The investor earns approximately $4,317 over 30 days on a ~$995,683 investment.
Exercise 9
Explain the difference between a competitive and a noncompetitive bid in a T-Bill auction. What is the maximum noncompetitive bid allowed?
Competitive bids specify both the desired par value and the discount yield the bidder requires; they may not be filled if the yield is too high relative to what the market clears at. They are used by large investors and dealers. Noncompetitive bids specify only the desired face value amount and accept whatever yield the auction produces — they are always filled. The maximum noncompetitive bid is $5 million for a single bidder.
Exercise 10
Bank A has $50M in excess reserves and calls its correspondent Bank B to offer an overnight fed funds loan. Bank B accepts at the fed funds rate of 5.33%. What is the dollar amount of interest Bank B pays for this one-day loan?
Fed funds are quoted on a simple interest basis. One day's interest = $50,000,000 × 0.0533 × (1/360) = $50,000,000 × 0.0001481 ≈ $7,403. Bank B pays approximately $7,403 in interest for the overnight use of $50M. (Note: fed funds use a 360-day convention.)
Tier 3 · Analysis
Critical thinking and comparative analysis
Exercise 11
📝 Compare repos and fed funds as short-term borrowing mechanisms. What are two advantages repos have over fed funds from a lender's perspective?
From a lender's perspective, repos have two key advantages over fed funds: (1) Collateralization — repos are backed by securities (usually T-Bills or agency bonds), so if the borrower defaults, the lender holds collateral. Fed funds loans are completely unsecured. (2) Haircut protection — the collateral in a repo exceeds the loan amount (a "haircut"), providing a buffer even if collateral value drops slightly. These protections make repos lower-risk, which is why repos typically trade at rates slightly below the fed funds rate. 📝 (Enriched beyond slides)
Exercise 12
Why would a corporation prefer to issue commercial paper rather than obtain a short-term bank loan, assuming it has a strong credit rating?
Companies with strong credit ratings can borrow at lower rates via CP than through bank loans because: (1) CP eliminates bank intermediation costs — banks profit from the spread between deposit rates and loan rates; (2) the CP market is competitive, so rates closely reflect market conditions; (3) large investors (money market funds, corporations) buy directly, cutting out the middleman. The tradeoff is that CP is unsecured, so only creditworthy companies can access the market — a company with weaker credit must pay higher yields or rely on bank lines of credit.
Exercise 13
Explain how the 2007–2008 financial crisis illustrated the danger of maturity transformation in the ABCP market. What structural mismatch caused the collapse?
The core problem was a maturity mismatch: ABCP vehicles funded long-duration, illiquid assets (30-year mortgage-backed securities) with short-term paper (20–45 day CP). This is called "maturity transformation." As long as investors rolled over (renewed) the CP at maturity, the vehicle could function. But when subprime mortgage defaults rose and MBS values collapsed, investors lost confidence and refused to roll over their CP. The ABCP vehicles suddenly had long-term illiquid assets but no short-term funding — a classic liquidity crisis. This shows why money market instruments must be backed by genuinely short-term, high-quality, liquid assets — not repackaged versions of risky long-term securities.
Exercise 14
A negotiable CD and a T-Bill both have 6-month maturities and similar yields. Why might a money market mutual fund prefer one over the other?
📝 Key considerations: T-Bills have essentially zero default risk (backed by the U.S. government), higher liquidity (largest secondary market of any money market security), and are used in the Fed's repo operations. Negotiable CDs carry slight bank credit risk (though low for large banks), but may offer slightly higher yields as compensation. A very conservative fund (e.g., a government money market fund) would prefer T-Bills. A prime money market fund might prefer CDs if the yield premium is sufficient. 📝 (Enriched beyond slides)
Exercise 15
The Federal Reserve wants to lower the fed funds rate. Using your knowledge of T-Bills and open market operations, explain the transmission mechanism by which open market purchases reduce the fed funds rate.
📝 The transmission mechanism works as follows: (1) The Fed's FOMC directs the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to purchase T-Bills from primary dealers in the open market. (2) The Fed pays for these T-Bills by crediting the dealers' reserve accounts at the Fed — this creates new reserves in the banking system. (3) With more reserves available, banks that hold excess reserves are eager to lend them overnight in the fed funds market. The supply of lendable reserves increases. (4) With supply up and demand roughly constant, the price of overnight reserves (the fed funds rate) falls. The rate drops toward the FOMC's new target. This is the primary channel through which monetary policy is transmitted to the broader economy. 📝 (Enriched beyond slides)
