Chapter Summary
Read this first — the foundation for everything else

Why Interest Rates Matter

Interest rates are among the most powerful forces in finance. They are the price of money — what you pay to borrow it and what you earn by lending it. Nominal interest rates are the rates actually observed in financial markets: the 5% on your savings account, the 7% on a mortgage, the 4% yield on a Treasury bond. These rates directly affect the price of virtually every financial security, and their movements ripple through every corner of the economy.

When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (because new bonds offer higher yields, making old ones less valuable). When rates fall, borrowing becomes cheaper, stimulating business investment and consumer spending. Central banks like the Federal Reserve spend enormous energy trying to understand, forecast, and influence interest rates — which is why understanding where interest rates come from is foundational to all of finance.

📝
Background: Nominal vs. Real interest rates A "nominal" rate is the rate you actually see quoted. A "real" rate adjusts for inflation. If your savings account pays 5% but inflation is 3%, your real return is approximately 2% — you're only gaining 2% in actual purchasing power. This distinction is critical throughout Chapter 2 and reappears constantly in finance.

The Loanable Funds Theory

The loanable funds theory is the foundational framework for understanding interest rate determination. It views equilibrium interest rates as the result of the supply of and demand for loanable funds in financial markets — just like the price of any good is determined by supply and demand. When supply of funds increases or demand decreases, interest rates fall. When demand increases or supply decreases, interest rates rise.

Suppliers of Loanable Funds

The "supply of loanable funds" describes capital provided to financial markets by those with surplus money. Supply increases as interest rates rise — higher rates incentivize saving and lending. The major suppliers are:

Supplier2019 AmountNotes
Household Sector$84.66 trillionLargest supplier; savings and investment accounts
Financial Businesses$98.47 trillionBanks, insurance cos, investment funds
Nonfinancial Businesses$28.06 trillionCorporations with excess cash (e.g., Apple's $200B+ cash pile)
Foreign Investors$27.20 trillionView U.S. markets as investment alternatives
Government$5.66 trillionSmallest supplier; occasionally runs surpluses

What Shifts the Supply Curve

At any given interest rate, the supply of loanable funds shifts when these factors change:

Shift Factor 1
Wealth of Suppliers

As overall wealth increases, people and institutions supply more funds. A growing economy generates more surplus capital looking for investment. Wealth ↑ → Supply ↑

Shift Factor 2
Risk of Securities

If financial securities become riskier (more volatility, more defaults), suppliers pull back. Risk ↑ → Supply ↓ (risk-averse investors demand higher rates before they'll supply funds)

Shift Factor 3
Near-Term Spending Needs

If households anticipate upcoming expenses (holiday season, medical costs), they save more beforehand. Spending needs ↑ → Supply ↑ (temporarily, as they accumulate funds)

Shift Factor 4
Monetary Policy

When the Federal Reserve allows economic expansion (e.g., low rates, bond purchases), it injects money into the system. Expansionary policy → Supply ↑. Contractionary policy → Supply ↓

📝
Shift Factor 5: Foreign economic conditions When domestic economic conditions improve, domestic suppliers have more to invest, increasing the local supply of funds. When foreign economies improve, foreign investors may redirect funds home rather than to U.S. markets — reducing the foreign supply of funds to U.S. markets. These are asymmetric effects worth remembering.

Demanders of Loanable Funds

The "demand for loanable funds" describes the total net demand for borrowing by fund users. Demand generally increases as interest rates fall — cheaper borrowing costs make more projects viable. The major borrowers are:

Borrower2019 AmountPurpose
Financial Businesses$111.87 trillionLargest borrower; funding loans, securities
Nonfinancial Businesses$66.46 trillionCapital investment, working capital
Government$28.86 trillionBudget deficits, infrastructure, defense
Households$16.05 trillionMortgages, auto loans, credit cards
Foreign Participants$20.81 trillionMostly business sector borrowing

What Shifts the Demand Curve

Three key factors shift demand at any given rate:

1. Utility/return from borrowed funds. If the expected return from an investment (funded by borrowed money) rises, businesses are willing to borrow more even at the same rate. Expected project return ↑ → Demand ↑.

2. Non-price conditions on borrowing. Even if the interest rate stays the same, if banks require more collateral, impose restrictive covenants (limits on what borrowers can do with funds), or charge higher fees, effective borrowing becomes more expensive. More restrictive non-price conditions → Demand ↓.

3. Economic growth vs. stagnation. During economic expansion, businesses borrow more to invest in new capacity and inventory. During recessions, borrowing plummets because investment opportunities look poor. Growth → Demand ↑; Stagnation → Demand ↓.

⚠️
Common misconception: Government as supplier vs. demander Students often forget that governments appear on BOTH sides of the loanable funds market. The government can supply funds (when running a surplus or managing reserves) AND demand funds (when running a deficit, which is most of the time in the U.S.). In practice, the U.S. government is a massive net borrower, so it primarily shifts the demand curve rightward, putting upward pressure on interest rates — a phenomenon called "crowding out."

Determinants of Interest Rates for Individual Securities

The loanable funds theory explains market-level interest rates. But why do different securities trade at different rates? A 10-year Treasury bond, a corporate junk bond, and a municipal bond all have different yields — even in the same market, at the same time. The answer lies in the components that make up any security's interest rate:

The Fair Interest Rate Equation
ij* = f(IP, RFR, DRPj, LRPj, SCPj, MPj)
ij* = fair (required) interest rate on security j
IP = inflation premium (expected inflation)
RFR = real risk-free rate
DRPj = default risk premium for security j
LRPj = liquidity risk premium for security j
SCPj = special covenant/feature premium for security j
MPj = maturity premium for security j

Each component compensates the investor for a specific type of cost or risk. Let's unpack each one.

1. Inflation Premium (IP) and the Fisher Effect

Inflation is the continual increase in the price level of a basket of goods and services. It is measured in the U.S. using the Consumer Price Index (CPI) or Producer Price Index (PPI). The key insight: if you lend money at 5% for a year, but prices rise 4%, your purchasing power only grew by 1%. Investors demand compensation for expected inflation — the inflation premium (IP).

The relationship between the real risk-free rate, expected inflation, and the nominal interest rate is captured by the Fisher effect, named after economist Irving Fisher:

Fisher Effect (Approximate)
i ≈ RFR + E(IP)
Nominal Rate ≈ Real Rate + Expected Inflation
The precise (exact) Fisher equation is: (1 + i) = (1 + RFR) × (1 + E(IP))
For low rates, the approximation works well. At higher rates, use the exact equation.
📊 Fisher Effect Example
1
Given: Real risk-free rate (RFR) = 2%, Expected inflation E(IP) = 3%
2
Approximate: i ≈ 2% + 3% = 5%
3
Exact: (1.02)(1.03) = 1.0506, so i = 5.06%
Nominal rate ≈ 5% | The extra 0.06% is the "cross product" — small at low rates, matters at high rates

The real risk-free rate (RFR) is the interest rate that would exist on a risk-free security if no inflation were expected. It represents society's "time preference for money" — how much people prefer consumption today over future consumption. Higher preference for spending now → higher RFR. The RFR is theoretical; in practice, the U.S. Treasury bill rate (adjusted for inflation) is used as a proxy.

2. Default Risk Premium (DRP)

Default risk is the risk that a security issuer will fail to make promised interest and principal payments. The higher the default risk, the higher the interest rate investors demand. The compensation for bearing this risk is the default risk premium (DRP):

Default Risk Premium
DRPj = ij − iT
ij = yield on security j (e.g., corporate bond)
iT = yield on Treasury security with same maturity, liquidity, and features
This spread is often called the "credit spread" and is a key signal of market risk appetite.
📝
Background: Credit rating agencies Companies like Moody's, Standard & Poor's (S&P), and Fitch assign credit ratings to bond issuers. AAA/Aaa rated bonds are considered investment-grade with very low default risk; BB/Ba and below are "junk" or high-yield bonds with higher default risk — and correspondingly higher DRPs. The spread between AAA corporate bonds and Treasury yields fluctuates with economic conditions, widening sharply during crises.

3. Liquidity Risk Premium (LRP)

Liquidity risk is the risk that a security cannot be sold quickly at a predictable price with low transaction costs. Highly liquid assets (like U.S. Treasury securities) trade constantly with tiny bid-ask spreads. Illiquid assets (like thinly traded municipal bonds) may require selling at a significant discount if you need cash quickly. Investors who accept illiquid securities demand a liquidity risk premium (LRP).

Think of it this way: a house and a U.S. Treasury bond might offer the same annual "return," but you'd demand extra compensation for the house because selling it takes months. That extra compensation is the liquidity premium.
⚠️
Two faces of liquidity risk premium LRP can arise in two ways: (1) actual illiquidity — the security is hard to trade; and (2) maturity-related sensitivity — even liquid long-term bonds are riskier than short-term bonds because their prices are more sensitive to interest rate changes. Your notes specifically mention this second source. A 30-year bond's price drops far more than a 1-year bond when rates rise by the same amount — this duration risk is sometimes compensated with an LRP.

4. Special Covenant/Feature Premium (SCP)

Certain contractual features written into a security's terms affect its interest rate. These provisions can either benefit or burden the investor:

FeatureWho BenefitsEffect on RateExample
Tax-exempt statusInvestorRate ↓ (investor accepts lower yield for tax savings)Municipal bonds — interest free from federal tax
ConvertibilityInvestorRate ↓ (option to convert to stock has value)Convertible bonds — can swap for issuer's equity
CallabilityIssuerRate ↑ (issuer can repay early, hurting investor if rates fall)Callable bonds — issuer can "call" them before maturity
Restrictive covenantsInvestorRate ↓ (restrictions protect bondholder)Limits on issuer's debt levels, asset sales
⚠️
Key rule to remember Features that benefit the investor → lower interest rate (investor accepts less yield for the added value). Features that benefit the issuer → higher interest rate (investor demands more yield to compensate for the disadvantage). Callability is the classic "issuer benefit" that students often forget raises the yield.

Term Structure of Interest Rates and the Yield Curve

The term structure of interest rates is a comparison of yields on securities that are identical in every way except their maturity. Plotted on a chart, this relationship is called the yield curve. The yield curve is one of the most important and closely watched tools in finance because its shape signals expectations about the economy and future interest rates.

The change in required interest rates as maturity changes is called the maturity premium (MP). The MP can be positive, negative, or zero — which gives rise to three common yield curve shapes:

Three Theories Explaining Yield Curve Shape

Theory 1
Unbiased Expectations Theory

Long-term rates equal the average of expected future short-term rates. If the market expects short-term rates to rise, long-term rates will be higher today — creating an upward slope. If rates are expected to fall, the curve inverts. This theory implies the yield curve reflects pure expectations, nothing more.

Theory 2
Liquidity Premium Theory

Extends expectations theory by adding a liquidity (risk) premium for longer maturities. Investors are risk-averse and dislike locking money up for long periods. So long-term rates = expected future short-term rates + a liquidity premium. This means yield curves will be upward biased even if rates are expected to stay flat.

Theory 3
Market Segmentation Theory

Different investors prefer different maturities (their "habitats") and won't easily move between maturity segments. Banks prefer short-term; pension funds prefer long-term. Supply and demand within each segment independently determines yields. The curve shape is driven by segment-specific supply/demand, not expectations.

⚠️
Why does an inverted yield curve matter so much? An inverted yield curve (short-term rates higher than long-term rates) is historically one of the most reliable predictors of a recession. It signals that the market expects the central bank to cut rates in the future (because the economy will weaken), making long-term rates lower than short-term rates today. Every U.S. recession since 1955 was preceded by an inverted yield curve.
📝
Expectations Theory Calculation: Example Suppose the 1-year rate today is 3%, the expected 1-year rate next year is 4%, and the expected 1-year rate in two years is 5%. Under the Unbiased Expectations Theory, the 3-year rate today should be approximately (3% + 4% + 5%) / 3 = 4%. The yield curve is upward-sloping because rates are expected to rise.

Time Value of Money (TVM)

The time value of money is the foundational concept underlying all of finance: a dollar received today is worth more than a dollar received in the future. Why? Because a dollar today can be invested to earn a return, making it worth more than a dollar received later. This creates a framework for comparing cash flows that occur at different points in time.

📝
The 3 Rules of Time Travel (from your TVM supplement) Rule 1: You can only compare/combine cash flows at the same point in time. Rule 2: To move a cash flow forward, you must compound it (multiply by (1+r)). Rule 3: To move a cash flow backward, you must discount it (divide by (1+r)). Everything in TVM follows from these three rules.

Lump Sum Formulas

Future Value of a Lump Sum
FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ
FV = future value (what your money grows to)
PV = present value (what you invest today)
r = interest rate per period (as decimal)
n = number of periods
Present Value of a Lump Sum
PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ
Also written as: PV = FV × (1 + r)⁻ⁿ
The term (1+r)⁻ⁿ is called the discount factor. "Discounting" means finding what a future sum is worth today.
📊 Lump Sum Examples

FV Example: Invest $1,000 today at 10% for 2 years:

1
FV = 1,000 × (1.10)² = 1,000 × 1.21 = $1,210

PV Example: Need $10,000 in 1 year. Can earn 7%. How much to invest today?

1
PV = 10,000 / (1.07)¹ = $9,345.79
2
Financial calculator: N=1, I/Y=7, FV=10000, PMT=0 → CPT PV = -9,345.79
Key insight: At higher discount rates, PV is smaller. At longer time horizons, PV is smaller.

Annuity Formulas

An annuity is a series of equal cash flows received at fixed intervals over a finite number of periods. Your mortgage payment, car payment, or annual bond coupon are all annuities.

Present Value of an Annuity
PV = C × [1 − 1/(1+r)ⁿ] / r
C = periodic cash flow (the annuity payment)
r = interest rate per period
n = number of periods
Calculator: Enter N, I/Y, PMT=C, FV=0 → CPT PV
Future Value of an Annuity
FV = C × [(1+r)ⁿ − 1] / r
Calculator: Enter N, I/Y, PMT=C, PV=0 → CPT FV
📊 Annuity Example: PV of Bond Coupons

A bond pays $50 per year for 3 years, then $1,000 at maturity. Discount rate = 6%. Find PV of the coupon payments.

1
PV of annuity = 50 × [1 − 1/(1.06)³] / 0.06
2
= 50 × [1 − 0.8396] / 0.06 = 50 × 2.6730 = $133.65
3
PV of $1,000 face value = 1,000 / (1.06)³ = $839.62
Total Bond Price = $133.65 + $839.62 = $973.27 (bond trades at discount because coupon rate < discount rate)

Perpetuity Formulas

A perpetuity is an annuity that pays forever. It sounds complex, but the formula is beautifully simple:

Present Value of a Perpetuity
PV = C / r
This works because in an infinite series, the compounding overwhelms the finite payment, leaving just C/r.
Present Value of a Growing Perpetuity
PV = C / (r − g)
g = constant growth rate of the cash flow (g must be < r)
This formula is critical for stock valuation (Gordon Growth Model: stock price = D₁/(r−g))
Present Value of a Growing Annuity
PV = C × [1 − ((1+g)/(1+r))ᴺ] / (r−g)
Growing annuity: finite payments that grow at rate g each period.

Solving for Rate (r) and Time (n)

Solving for Discount Rate (r)
r = (FV/PV)^(1/t) − 1
Calculator: Enter N, PV (negative), FV, PMT → CPT I/Y
Solving for Number of Periods (n)
t = ln(FV/PV) / ln(1+r)
Calculator: Enter I/Y, PV (negative), FV, PMT → CPT N
Rule of 72 (Quick Mental Math)
Time to double ≈ 72 / interest rate (%)
Example: At 9% interest, your money doubles in approximately 72/9 = 8 years.
Works in reverse: if money doubles in 8 years, the interest rate ≈ 72/8 = 9%.
⚠️
Critical: Simple vs. Compound Interest Simple interest earns interest only on the principal. Compound interest earns interest on principal PLUS previously earned interest. At $100 × 5% for 2 years: Simple FV = $110. Compound FV = $110.25. The difference grows enormously over long periods. At 200 years: Simple = $1,200. Compound = $4,471,898. Every finance problem assumes compound interest unless explicitly told otherwise.

Calculator sign convention (critical for avoiding errors) On the BA-II Plus, cash outflows are negative, inflows are positive. If you invest $1,000 today (outflow), enter PV = −1,000. The calculator will return FV as a positive number (you receive it). Always clear TVM registers (2ND → FV) between problems. Forgetting the sign convention is the #1 cause of calculator errors on exams.

Why Does Compound Interest Grow So Explosively?
Simple Explanation

Imagine a snowball rolling downhill. The bigger it gets, the more snow it picks up — so it keeps accelerating. Compound interest works the same way. Each year, you earn interest not just on your original money, but also on all the interest you've already earned. So the pile grows, which means next year's interest is even bigger, which makes the pile even bigger, and so on forever.

Put $100 at 10%. After year 1: $110. After year 2: not $120 (simple) but $121 — because you earned 10% on $110, not just $100. That extra $1 seems trivial. But over 30 years, your $100 becomes $1,745 with compounding, versus $400 with simple interest. The snowball effect is enormous.

Where the analogy breaks down

A snowball eventually gets too big to roll and stops. Money, in theory, can compound forever without stopping (though inflation, taxes, and market returns vary in practice). Also, compounding requires reinvesting returns — if you spend your interest each year, it's essentially simple interest.

The precise version

Compound interest applies the interest rate to the growing accumulated balance, not just the original principal. The future value formula is FV = PV × (1+r)ⁿ. The exponent n is what creates the exponential (not linear) growth. Simple interest grows as FV = PV × (1 + r×n), which is linear. The mathematical difference between linear and exponential growth is what makes compounding so powerful over long horizons.

The Fisher Effect: Why Inflation Drives Interest Rates
Simple Explanation

Imagine you lend your friend $100 so they can buy you pizza when they repay you next year. But you know pizza will cost 10% more next year due to inflation. If your friend just gives back $100, you can buy less pizza than you could today. So you'd tell your friend: "Pay me $110 — $100 to replace what I lent you, and $10 to cover the fact that pizza costs more now."

That extra $10 is the inflation premium. Lenders in financial markets do exactly this: they add expected inflation to their required return so they don't lose purchasing power. If I want a 2% real return and expect 3% inflation, I'll demand 5% nominal.

Where the analogy breaks down

The real world is more complex because the actual inflation that occurs may differ from expected inflation. Lenders are compensated for expected inflation at the time of the loan; if actual inflation turns out higher, they lose purchasing power anyway. Also, the Fisher effect is empirically approximate — the precise relationship involves a multiplicative cross-product term that matters at high inflation rates.

The precise version

The Fisher effect states: (1+i) = (1+RFR) × (1+E(IP)), where i is the nominal rate, RFR is the real risk-free rate, and E(IP) is expected inflation. The approximation i ≈ RFR + E(IP) ignores the cross-product term RFR×E(IP), which is small at low rates but significant at high rates. The Fisher effect implies that nominal rates should rise one-for-one with changes in expected inflation, holding the real rate constant — an empirically tested prediction with strong (but imperfect) support in the data.

3
The Yield Curve: What Its Shape Is Telling You
Simple Explanation

Imagine there's a rumor that next summer will be extremely hot. Water vendors right now charge $1 per bottle (that's the current "short-term price"). But vendors willing to lock in a price for next summer are charging $3 per bottle because of the expected shortage. That "forward price" is higher because people expect conditions to change.

The yield curve works similarly. If people expect interest rates to rise in the future (because the economy will be booming), they demand higher yields today on long-term loans. Why? Because if rates rise in two years, a long-term bond paying today's rate will look stingy. The curve slopes upward. If people expect a recession (rates to fall), the curve may invert — short-term rates are high now but expected to fall.

Where the analogy breaks down

The yield curve reflects multiple forces simultaneously, not just expectations: risk premiums for holding long-term bonds, segmented market supply/demand, Federal Reserve policy interventions, and global capital flows. No single theory fully explains all yield curve shapes at all times — that's why three competing theories exist.

The precise version

The term structure of interest rates plots yields against maturities for securities with identical characteristics except maturity. The maturity premium (MP) can be positive, negative, or zero. Three theories explain its shape: (1) Unbiased Expectations — long rates equal the geometric average of expected future short rates; (2) Liquidity Premium — long rates include a risk premium above expectations; (3) Market Segmentation — supply and demand in each maturity "habitat" independently determines yields, with no arbitrage across segments.

Present Value: Why Future Money Is Worth Less Today
Simple Explanation

Suppose someone offers you a choice: $1,000 right now, or a guaranteed $1,000 exactly one year from now. Any rational person takes the money now — because you can invest it and have more than $1,000 in a year. The future $1,000 is worth less to you today because you're giving up the ability to earn a return on it.

To compare them fairly, we need to ask: "How much would I need to invest today to have $1,000 in one year?" If you can earn 10%, the answer is $1,000 ÷ 1.10 = $909.09. So the present value of $1,000 received in one year at 10% is $909.09. We "discounted" the future value back to today's terms.

Where the analogy breaks down

This assumes certainty — guaranteed future payments. In reality, future cash flows are usually uncertain. The discount rate used in practice incorporates not just the risk-free rate but also risk premiums to account for that uncertainty. Riskier cash flows get discounted at higher rates, making their present value lower — reflecting that investors demand compensation for uncertainty.

The precise version

Present value (PV) is the current value of a future cash flow, discounted at an appropriate rate. For a lump sum: PV = FV/(1+r)ⁿ. For a stream of cash flows: PV = Σ Cₙ/(1+r)ⁿ. The discount rate r represents the opportunity cost of capital — the return available on the best alternative investment of comparable risk. Higher r → lower PV. Longer horizon → lower PV. This framework underlies all of security valuation, capital budgeting, and financial decision-making.

Tier 1 Recall — Core concepts and definitions
🟢 Q1 · Basic
What does the loanable funds theory say determines equilibrium interest rates in financial markets?
Answer

Equilibrium interest rates are determined by the supply of and demand for loanable funds in financial markets — exactly like price determination in a product market. When supply of funds exceeds demand, rates fall; when demand exceeds supply, rates rise. The theory categorizes all market participants (consumers, businesses, governments, foreign participants) as either net suppliers or demanders of funds.

🟢 Q2 · Basic
Name and briefly describe the 5 components that determine the fair interest rate on an individual security (the ij* equation).
Answer

ij* = f(IP, RFR, DRPj, LRPj, SCPj, MPj)

(1) IP — Inflation Premium: compensates for expected loss in purchasing power
(2) RFR — Real Risk-Free Rate: base return for time preference with no risk/inflation
(3) DRP — Default Risk Premium: compensates for risk of issuer failing to pay
(4) LRP — Liquidity Risk Premium: compensates for difficulty selling at fair price quickly
(5) SCP — Special Covenant Premium: adjusts for features like callability (↑ rate) or tax-exemption (↓ rate)
(6) MP — Maturity Premium: accounts for sensitivity to rate changes with longer term

🟢 Q3 · Basic
You invest $1,000 at 8% for 3 years. What is the future value? What would it be with simple interest?
Answer

Compound FV = PV × (1+r)ⁿ = 1,000 × (1.08)³ = 1,000 × 1.2597 = $1,259.71

Simple FV = PV × (1 + r×n) = 1,000 × (1 + 0.08×3) = 1,000 × 1.24 = $1,240.00

Difference: $19.71. This gap grows significantly over longer horizons — compound interest's "snowball effect" becomes increasingly powerful over time.

🟢 Q4 · Basic
Using the Rule of 72, approximately how long does it take to double your money at 6% interest? At 12%?
Answer

At 6%: 72 / 6 = 12 years
At 12%: 72 / 12 = 6 years

Doubling the interest rate cuts the doubling time in half. Check: $1,000 at 6% for 12 years = 1,000 × (1.06)¹² = $2,012 ✓ (approximately doubles). The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math shortcut — handy on exams when you need a rough estimate without a calculator.

🟢 Q5 · Basic
A perpetuity pays $500 per year forever. If the required return is 5%, what is its present value?
Answer

PV = C / r = 500 / 0.05 = $10,000

This is elegantly simple: a stream of $500 per year forever, discounted at 5%, is worth $10,000 today. Makes intuitive sense — $10,000 invested at 5% generates $500/year in interest, so $10,000 is the fair price for that cash flow stream.

Tier 2 Application — Calculations and scenario analysis
🟡 Q6 · Intermediate
Using the Fisher effect: If the real risk-free rate is 1.5% and expected inflation is 2.8%, what is the nominal interest rate (both approximate and exact)?
Answer

Approximate: i ≈ RFR + E(IP) = 1.5% + 2.8% = 4.3%

Exact (Fisher): (1+i) = (1.015)(1.028) = 1.04342, so i = 4.342%

The cross-product (0.015 × 0.028 = 0.000042 = 0.042%) is very small at these rate levels, so the approximation works well. At 20% inflation and 5% real rate, the difference would be 1% — much more significant.

🟡 Q7 · Intermediate
You want to save $150,000 for your child's college education in 17 years. You can earn 8% per year. How much must you invest today? Calculator inputs?
Answer

Formula: PV = FV / (1+r)ⁿ = 150,000 / (1.08)¹⁷

(1.08)¹⁷ = 3.7000 (approx)

PV = 150,000 / 3.7000 = $40,540.34

Calculator: N=17, I/Y=8, FV=150,000, PMT=0 → CPT PV = -40,540.34

Investing ~$40,500 today at 8% grows to $150,000 in 17 years. This is the power of early saving and compound growth.

🟡 Q8 · Intermediate
A car loan requires monthly payments of $350 for 48 months at an annual rate of 6% (0.5%/month). What is the loan amount (PV of the annuity)?
Answer

Formula: PV = C × [1 − 1/(1+r)ⁿ] / r

= 350 × [1 − 1/(1.005)⁴⁸] / 0.005

(1.005)⁴⁸ = 1.2705; 1/1.2705 = 0.7871; 1 − 0.7871 = 0.2129

= 350 × 0.2129 / 0.005 = 350 × 42.58 = $14,904.22

Calculator: N=48, I/Y=0.5, PMT=350, FV=0 → CPT PV = -14,904.22

Note: When payments are monthly, the period interest rate must be monthly (6%/12 = 0.5%) and N is the number of months, not years.

🟡 Q9 · Intermediate
A 10-year AAA corporate bond yields 4.5%. A 10-year U.S. Treasury yields 3.8%. What is the default risk premium? If the same AAA bond had a callability feature, would the yield be higher or lower, and why?
Answer

DRP = Corporate yield − Treasury yield = 4.5% − 3.8% = 0.70% (70 basis points)

The DRP compensates investors for the (small, since AAA) risk that the corporation might default, compared to the effectively zero-default risk of the U.S. Treasury.

Callability: The yield would be HIGHER. A callable bond gives the issuer the right to repay early — typically when rates fall. This hurts the investor (who loses the above-market coupon just when they'd most want it). Investors demand a higher yield (call premium) to compensate for this disadvantage. Callable bonds always have higher yields than equivalent non-callable bonds.

🟡 Q10 · Intermediate
Under the Unbiased Expectations Theory: The 1-year rate today is 4%. The market expects the 1-year rate to be 5% next year and 6% the year after. What should the 3-year rate be today?
Answer

Exact calculation: (1 + 3yr rate)³ = (1.04)(1.05)(1.06) = 1.15668

3yr rate = (1.15668)^(1/3) − 1 = 1.04993 − 1 = 4.993% ≈ 5%

Approximation: (4% + 5% + 6%) / 3 = 5%

The 3-year yield today (≈5%) is higher than the current 1-year rate (4%) because the market expects rates to rise. This creates an upward-sloping yield curve at these maturities.

Tier 3 Synthesis — Integration and analysis
🔴 Q11 · Advanced
The Federal Reserve raises interest rates significantly to combat high inflation. Using the loanable funds framework, explain: (a) what happens to supply of funds, (b) what happens to demand for funds, (c) what does this tell us about the Fed's strategy?
Answer

(a) Supply of funds: Higher interest rates incentivize more saving (higher return for deferring consumption). Supply curve shifts rightward — more funds available at each rate level. Foreign investors may also move funds into U.S. markets for higher yields, increasing supply further.

(b) Demand for funds: Higher rates make borrowing more expensive. Businesses postpone investments that no longer meet return thresholds (projects must earn more than the higher borrowing cost). Household demand for mortgages and consumer credit falls. Demand curve shifts leftward — fewer funds demanded at each rate level.

(c) Fed's strategy: By tightening monetary policy, the Fed is deliberately reducing the supply of reserves and raising rates, which: (1) reduces spending/investment (cools the economy); (2) reduces the demand for funds; and (3) works through the Fisher effect — by bringing actual inflation down, it reduces the inflation premium component of all interest rates, eventually allowing nominal rates to fall. The mechanism is intentional monetary contraction to reduce aggregate demand and cool price pressures.

🔴 Q12 · Advanced
Compare and contrast the three theories of the term structure. Which theory best explains why yield curves almost always slope upward, even when interest rates aren't expected to rise?
Answer

Unbiased Expectations Theory: Long-term rates = average of expected future short-term rates. Predicts flat curves when rates are expected to stay flat, inverted when expected to fall. Assumes risk-neutral investors who are indifferent between short and long maturities.

Liquidity Premium Theory: Extends expectations by adding a positive risk premium for longer maturities. Even if rates are expected to stay flat, the curve slopes upward because investors demand extra return for holding longer-term, less liquid securities. Explains why curves are usually upward-sloping.

Market Segmentation Theory: Investors and institutions prefer specific maturities based on their liability structures. Banks prefer short-term (matching short-term deposits); pension funds prefer long-term (matching long-term obligations). Supply and demand within each segment determines yields independently. The shape reflects segment-specific imbalances, not expectations.

Best explanation for persistent upward slope: Liquidity Premium Theory. It preserves the expectations logic while adding a realistic behavioral element: investors are risk-averse and demand compensation for holding longer, more volatile securities. This naturally biases the curve upward even when rates are expected to be flat — which matches empirical data far better than the pure expectations theory.

🔴 Q13 · Advanced
Municipal bonds (munis) typically yield less than corporate bonds of equivalent maturity and credit quality. Using the interest rate determinants framework, explain precisely why — and what type of investor munis are best suited for.
Answer

Why munis yield less: Municipal bond interest is exempt from federal income tax (and often state/local taxes in the issuing state). This is a "special feature" (SCP) that benefits the investor. Under the SCP framework, features that benefit investors → lower interest rate. Investors accept a lower nominal yield on munis because their after-tax return can equal or exceed a higher-yielding taxable bond's after-tax return.

Calculation illustration: An investor in the 35% tax bracket compares a 4% muni to a 6% corporate bond. After-tax corporate return = 6% × (1−0.35) = 3.9%. After-tax muni return = 4% (no tax). The muni wins despite the lower stated yield. The tax-equivalent yield formula: Tax-equiv yield = Muni yield / (1 − tax rate) = 4% / 0.65 = 6.15%. So the muni is equivalent to a 6.15% taxable bond.

Best investor for munis: High-income investors in high tax brackets — they gain the most from the tax exemption. Low-income investors (low tax bracket) benefit less from tax exemption and may find taxable bonds better. Tax-exempt institutional investors (pension funds, IRAs) gain nothing from the exemption and should avoid munis entirely.

🔴 Q14 · Advanced
A growing perpetuity pays $100 next year and is expected to grow at 3% per year forever. The required return is 8%. (a) What is the PV? (b) What happens to PV if the growth rate increases to 5%? (c) What if the required return falls to 5% while growth stays at 3%?
Answer

(a) PV = C/(r−g) = 100/(0.08−0.03) = 100/0.05 = $2,000

(b) If g rises to 5%: PV = 100/(0.08−0.05) = 100/0.03 = $3,333
Higher growth rate → smaller denominator → much higher PV. A 2% increase in growth rate increased value by 67%!

(c) If r falls to 5% (g stays at 3%): PV = 100/(0.05−0.03) = 100/0.02 = $5,000
The denominator (r−g) narrows from 0.05 to 0.02, more than doubling the value. This shows why falling interest rates cause massive increases in asset prices — they reduce the discount rate that shrinks all future cash flows.

Warning: If r ≤ g, the formula breaks down (you get zero or negative denominator). This is theoretically impossible for a real asset — in practice, growth rates must be below the discount rate in the long run.

🔴 Q15 · Advanced
A friend says: "With interest rates so high, this is a bad time to invest in long-term bonds." Using the concepts of default risk premium, liquidity risk premium, maturity premium, and the relationship between prices and yields, explain whether this is good financial reasoning and what risks your friend should consider.
Answer

Your friend has a point, but it's more nuanced:

Why they may be right (current risk): When rates are high, long-term bond prices are low (inverse relationship). If you buy now and rates rise further, bond prices fall more — and long-term bonds fall far more than short-term bonds for the same rate increase (this is the maturity premium / duration effect). If you need to sell before maturity, you could face capital losses.

Why they may be wrong (opportunity): High rates today mean high yields — you're locking in a high return for many years. If rates fall (which often happens after rate hikes), your bond's price rises and you can sell at a capital gain. Historically, after the Fed tightens aggressively, rates eventually fall, creating substantial capital gains on long-term bonds.

Additional risks to consider: DRP — is the bond's issuer creditworthy? During rate-hike cycles, economic stress can increase default risk on corporate bonds. LRP — can you sell the bond quickly if needed? Long-term corporate bonds can be illiquid in stressed markets. Reinvestment risk — if rates fall, future coupon reinvestment earns less.

Conclusion: Whether it's a "bad time" depends on your investment horizon and whether you hold to maturity. If held to maturity, interest rate risk is irrelevant (you get par value + coupons regardless). If you might need to sell early, high-rate environments are indeed risky for long-term bonds.

  • DRP Yield − equivalent Treasury yield. Higher for lower-rated issuers

  • SCP Tax-exempt → rate ↓. Callable → rate ↑. Convertible → rate ↓

📉 Yield Curve Quick Reference
  • Normal ↑ Long-term rates > short-term. Rates expected to rise. Most common shape.
  • Inverted ↓ Short-term > long-term. Rates expected to fall. Predicts recession.
  • Flat → All maturities similar yield. Transition between normal and inverted.
  • Expectations Long rate = avg of expected future short rates. Risk neutral.
  • Liquidity Prem Adds risk premium to expectations. Biases curve upward.
  • Market Seg Each maturity independently priced by its supply/demand segment.
