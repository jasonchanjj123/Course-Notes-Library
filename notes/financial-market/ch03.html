The Big Picture: Valuing Financial Securities

Chapter 3 is where the theoretical foundation of Chapters 1 and 2 becomes practical. You now know that interest rates exist and how they're determined — the question becomes: given an interest rate, what is a financial security actually worth? The answer always comes back to the same principle: the fair value of any financial security is the present value of all the cash flows it is expected to generate, discounted at the appropriate required rate of return. Everything in this chapter is an application of that one idea.

· · ·

Three Interest Rate Measures: Required, Expected, Realized

Before diving into valuation, your course distinguishes three different "interest rates" on a security. Students often confuse these, but they are conceptually distinct and describe different moments in time:

Measure 1
Required Rate of Return (r)
Ex Ante (Before)

The rate you should earn given the security's risk. Used to calculate fair present value. Reflects default risk, liquidity risk, maturity, and all other risk factors from Chapter 2. If market price = fair value, then r = E(r).

Measure 2
Expected Rate of Return E(r)
Ex Ante (Before)

The rate you expect to earn if you buy the security at its current market price, receive all projected cash flows, and sell at your horizon. This is the internal rate of return implied by current market price. Also called yield to maturity (YTM) for bonds.

Measure 3
Realized Rate of Return (r̄)
Ex Post (After)

The rate you actually earned on an investment that has already happened. May differ from required return because of unexpected events — price changes, defaults, early calls. If r̄ > r, you earned more than required; if r̄ < r, you underperformed.

Market Efficiency: Why Required ≈ Expected

In an efficient market, prices adjust quickly to new information so that current market price ≈ fair present value. This means E(r) ≈ r most of the time — you can't consistently find securities where the expected return exceeds the required return (that would be a "free lunch"). When unexpected events occur (surprise earnings, Fed rate changes, credit downgrades), prices temporarily diverge from fair value, then quickly converge again as traders act on the mispricing.

📝
Background: Why this distinction matters In bond markets, E(r) = YTM is computed from market price. In stock markets, E(r) is harder to compute because dividends are uncertain. When a bond's YTM (E(r)) is higher than its required rate (r), the bond is underpriced — a buying opportunity. When YTM < r, the bond is overpriced. Market efficiency says such gaps close quickly as investors exploit them.

Bond Valuation

A bond is a debt instrument where the issuer (borrower) promises to pay the holder (lender): (1) periodic coupon payments (interest) over the life of the bond, and (2) the face value (par value) — typically $1,000 — at maturity. The fair price of a bond equals the present value of all these promised cash flows, discounted at the required rate of return.

Coupon Bonds

Bond Price (Coupon Bond)
P = INT × [1 − 1/(1+r_b)ᴺ] / r_b + M / (1+r_b)ᴺ
P = fair present value (price) of the bond
INT = periodic coupon payment = Coupon Rate × Face Value / periods per year
r_b = required rate of return per period
N = total number of periods (years × periods per year)
M = face/par value (typically $1,000)
First term = PV of the annuity of coupons. Second term = PV of the face value (lump sum). Both discounted at r_b.
📝
Semiannual compounding (most U.S. bonds) Most U.S. bonds pay coupons twice per year. For semiannual bonds: use r_b/2 as the period rate, and 2N as the number of periods. Coupon payment = (Coupon Rate × Face Value) / 2. Always check whether a problem specifies annual or semiannual compounding — it matters.
📊 Coupon Bond Pricing Example

A bond has a 8% coupon rate, $1,000 face value, 3 years to maturity, annual payments, and required return = 10%.

1
Annual coupon INT = 0.08 × $1,000 = $80
2
PV of coupons = 80 × [1 − 1/(1.10)³] / 0.10 = 80 × 2.4869 = $198.95
3
PV of face value = 1,000 / (1.10)³ = 1,000 / 1.331 = $751.31
Bond Price = $198.95 + $751.31 = $950.26 → Trades at a DISCOUNT (r_b = 10% > coupon rate 8%)

Zero-Coupon Bonds

A zero-coupon bond (also called a pure discount bond) makes no periodic coupon payments — INT = 0. You buy it at a discount and receive only the face value at maturity. The entire return comes from the price appreciation. U.S. Treasury Bills are the best-known example.

Zero-Coupon Bond Price & YTM
P = M / (1 + YTM)ⁿ
YTM = (M / P)^(1/n) − 1
M = face/par value at maturity
n = years to maturity
Zero-coupon bonds always trade at a discount (P < M) because no interim cash flows are received.
📊 Zero-Coupon Bond Example

A zero-coupon bond with $100,000 face value is priced at $96,618.36 with 1-year maturity. What is the YTM?

1
YTM = (100,000 / 96,618.36)^(1/1) − 1
2
= (1.035) − 1 = 3.5%
The investor earns $3,381.64 in profit (the discount), which equals a 3.5% return on the $96,618.36 investment.

Premium, Discount, and Par Bonds

The relationship between a bond's coupon rate and required rate of return determines whether it trades at a premium, discount, or par. This is one of the most tested relationships in the chapter:

Bond Price vs. Coupon Rate Relationship
Scenario A
Coupon Rate < Required Rate
P < $1,000
DISCOUNT BOND

Bond pays less than market demands → price falls below par. YTM > coupon rate.

Scenario B
Coupon Rate = Required Rate
P = $1,000
PAR BOND

Bond pays exactly what market requires → trades at face value. YTM = coupon rate.

Scenario C
Coupon Rate > Required Rate
P > $1,000
PREMIUM BOND

Bond pays more than market requires → price rises above par. YTM < coupon rate.

Yield to Maturity (YTM)

The Yield to Maturity (YTM) is the single discount rate that equates the present value of all promised cash flows to the current market price. It is the bond's expected rate of return if: (1) held to maturity, (2) all coupons are reinvested at the YTM rate. YTM is solved iteratively (trial and error) or by financial calculator:

YTM (Solve for r_b in the bond price equation)
P = INT × [1−1/(1+YTM)ᴺ]/YTM + M/(1+YTM)ᴺ
Calculator: Enter N, PV = −P (negative, you pay it), PMT = INT, FV = M → CPT I/Y = YTM
If YTM > coupon rate → bond at discount. If YTM < coupon rate → bond at premium.
⚠️
YTM assumption: reinvestment at YTM rate YTM assumes all coupon payments are reinvested at the YTM rate until maturity. If you actually reinvest at a different rate, your realized return will differ from YTM. This "reinvestment risk" is a key limitation of YTM as a measure of actual bond return.

Equity (Stock) Valuation

Valuing stocks is conceptually similar to valuing bonds — find the present value of all future cash flows. The difference: bond cash flows (coupons + face value) are contractually fixed. Stock cash flows (dividends) are uncertain, can grow, and theoretically continue forever. The framework for this is the Dividend Discount Model (DDM).

The price of any stock equals the present value of all future dividends it will pay, discounted at the equity cost of capital (r_E). P₀ = Σ Div_t / (1+r_E)^t for t = 1 to ∞

Since we can't enumerate infinite dividends, we use simplifying assumptions about dividend growth patterns:

Case 1: Zero Dividend Growth (Perpetuity)

Dividends remain constant forever — like a preferred stock with a fixed dividend:

Zero Growth Model
P₀ = Div / r_E
Div = constant annual dividend (forever)
r_E = required return on equity
This is just the perpetuity formula from Chapter 2: P = C/r. Stock with no growth acts like a perpetuity.

Case 2: Constant Growth (Gordon Growth Model)

Dividends grow at a constant rate g forever — appropriate for mature, stable companies:

Constant Growth Dividend Discount Model (Gordon Growth Model)
P₀ = Div₁ / (r_E − g)
r_E = Div₁/P₀ + g = Dividend Yield + Capital Gain Rate
Div₁ = next year's expected dividend = Div₀ × (1+g)
g = constant dividend growth rate (must be < r_E)
r_E = required return on equity (equity cost of capital)
The total return equation splits into: dividend yield (Div₁/P₀) + expected capital appreciation (g).
📊 Gordon Growth Model Example

A stock just paid a dividend of $2.00 (Div₀). Dividends grow at 5% forever. Required return = 10%.

1
Next dividend: Div₁ = $2.00 × 1.05 = $2.10
2
Stock price: P₀ = $2.10 / (0.10 − 0.05) = $2.10 / 0.05 = $42.00
3
Dividend yield = 2.10/42.00 = 5%; Capital gain rate = 5%; Total return = 10% ✓
Stock is worth $42. Total return = 5% dividend yield + 5% capital growth = 10% required return.
⚠️
Critical: g must be less than r_E If g ≥ r_E, the Gordon Growth Model gives a nonsensical result (zero or negative denominator → infinite or negative price). This is impossible in equilibrium. The long-run growth rate of any company must be less than the economy's growth rate, which in turn must be less than the discount rate. When using this model, always verify g < r_E.

Case 3: Supernormal (Nonconstant) Dividend Growth

Many companies — especially young, fast-growing firms — go through a period of high (above-normal) dividend growth before settling into steady-state growth. The three-step process handles this:

3-Step Supernormal Growth Process
Finding P₀ with Non-Constant Growth

Step 1: Calculate each dividend during the high-growth period and find its PV individually: PV(Divₜ) = Divₜ / (1+r_E)^t. Sum these PVs.


Step 2: Find the stock price at the END of the high-growth period (Pₙ) using the Gordon Growth Model with the stable long-run growth rate: Pₙ = Divₙ₊₁ / (r_E − g_stable). Then discount Pₙ back to today: PV(Pₙ) = Pₙ / (1+r_E)ⁿ.


Step 3: P₀ = Sum of PVs from Step 1 + PV(Pₙ) from Step 2.

📊 Supernormal Growth Example

A stock pays no dividend for 2 years, then Div₃ = $1.00 growing at 25% for 2 years (Div₄ = $1.25), then constant 6% forever. r_E = 12%.

1
Step 1 PVs: PV(Div₃) = 1.00/1.12³ = $0.712; PV(Div₄) = 1.25/1.12⁴ = $0.794
2
Div₅ = $1.25 × 1.06 = $1.325. Terminal price P₄ = 1.325/(0.12−0.06) = $22.08
3
PV(P₄) = 22.08/1.12⁴ = $14.03
P₀ = $0.712 + $0.794 + $14.03 = $15.54
📝
Total Return Components For any stock: r_E = Dividend Yield + Capital Gain Rate, where Dividend Yield = Div₁/P₀ and Capital Gain Rate = (P₁−P₀)/P₀. In the constant-growth model, capital gain rate = g, which is why g also represents price appreciation. Investors receive value both from dividends paid and from price appreciation over time.

Impact of Interest Rate Changes on Bond Prices

The relationship between interest rates and bond prices is one of the most fundamental in fixed income: when interest rates rise, bond prices fall; when rates fall, prices rise. This inverse relationship has three important nuances:

1. The Relationship Is Non-Linear (Convex)

As interest rates increase, bond prices decrease — but at a decreasing rate. This means a 1% rate increase reduces prices by less than a 1% rate decrease raises them. The price-rate curve is bowed (convex), not a straight line. This non-linearity is measured by convexity (covered below).

2. Maturity Effect: Longer Bonds Are More Sensitive

The further a bond is from maturity, the more its price changes for a given interest rate move. A 30-year bond's price falls far more when rates rise 1% than a 2-year bond's price. However, this relationship is also non-linear — increasing maturity increases price sensitivity, but at a decreasing rate. Going from 1 year to 2 years adds more sensitivity than going from 29 to 30 years.

3. Coupon Rate Effect: Lower-Coupon Bonds Are More Sensitive

The higher the coupon rate, the less price-sensitive the bond. Why? Higher coupons return more cash to investors early (sooner in the bond's life). Since early cash flows are less affected by distant rate changes, high-coupon bonds are "weighted" toward the near future — making them less sensitive. Zero-coupon bonds have the highest price sensitivity of any bond with a given maturity.

FactorDirectionEffect on Price Sensitivity
Maturity ↑Increases sensitivityLonger time → more future cash flows affected by rate change
Coupon Rate ↑Decreases sensitivityMore early cash flows → weighted average life is shorter
Interest Rate ↑Decreases sensitivityHigher rates discount distant flows more → duration falls

Duration: The Unified Measure of Price Sensitivity

Duration is the weighted-average time to maturity of a bond, using the relative present values of cash flows as weights. It is simultaneously: (1) a measure of the average life of a bond; and (2) a direct measure of the bond's price sensitivity to interest rate changes.

Duration Formula (Annual Compounding)
D = [Σ t × CF_t / (1+r)^t] / P
t = time period of cash flow
CF_t = cash flow at time t (coupon or coupon + face)
r = required rate of return (or YTM)
P = current bond price (= Σ CF_t/(1+r)^t)
Each cash flow's "weight" is its PV share of the total bond price. Duration is the time-weighted average of those weights.
Duration: Economic Meaning (Price Change Approximation)
ΔP/P ≈ −D × Δr / (1+r) [Annual compounding]
ΔP/P ≈ −D × Δ(r/2) / (1+r/2) [Semiannual compounding]
ΔP/P ≈ −MD × Δr
MD = Modified Duration = D / (1+r) or D / (1+r/2) for semiannual
Δr = change in interest rate (in decimal form)
A bond with duration of 5 years will fall approximately 5% in value for each 1% rise in interest rates (using modified duration).

Key Duration Features

Feature 1
Duration & Coupon Rate

Higher coupon → shorter duration. Higher coupons return cash sooner, shifting the weight of cash flows toward the near future. Zero-coupon bond: duration = maturity (the longest possible for any given maturity).

Feature 2
Duration & Rate of Return

Higher required return → shorter duration. Higher discount rates reduce the relative weight of distant cash flows (the face value). This means higher-rate environments produce lower duration bonds.

Feature 3
Duration & Maturity

Longer maturity → longer duration, but at a decreasing rate. As maturity increases, duration grows but asymptotically approaches a ceiling. For a perpetuity: D = (1+r)/r.

Feature 4
Zero-Coupon Bond Duration

For a zero-coupon bond: Duration = Maturity. There's only one cash flow (face value at maturity), so the weighted average time is simply the maturity. Zero-coupon bonds have the maximum duration for their maturity.

Convexity: When Duration Isn't Enough

Duration provides a linear approximation of how bond prices change with interest rates. But the actual price-rate relationship is curved (non-linear). Convexity is the degree of curvature of the price-interest rate curve around some interest rate level. It measures how much the duration approximation errors as rates change.

Two critical insights about convexity:

Duration overestimates price losses when rates rise and underestimates price gains when rates fall. The actual price is always better than the duration-predicted price — which means convexity is always good for the holder.

Property 1
Convexity Is Desirable

Greater convexity means more protection against rate increases (price falls less than duration predicts) and greater gains when rates fall (price rises more). Investors should prefer higher-convexity bonds, all else equal.

Property 2
Convexity Causes Duration Error

The larger the interest rate change, the more convexity causes duration's linear approximation to misestimate the actual price change. For small moves (a few basis points), duration is accurate. For large moves (100+ bps), use both duration and convexity.

Property 3
All Fixed-Income Securities Are Convex

Every bond's price-rate relationship is convex because bond prices are the sum of discounted cash flows — a non-linear function of the discount rate. Convexity is not optional; it's a mathematical property of all bonds.

A Basis Point
Unit of Measurement

Duration is most accurate for "small" changes — defined as one basis point = 0.01% (one-hundredth of 1%). For changes of a few basis points, duration alone is fine. For changes of 1% or more, convexity correction becomes important.

Why Do Bond Prices Move Opposite to Interest Rates?
Simple Explanation

Imagine you lent your friend $1,000 for a year, and they promised to pay you back $1,050 (5% interest). Now imagine the next day, everyone else starts lending money at 10% interest. Your friend's promise to pay you $1,050 is worth less than a fresh 10% loan — if someone else wanted to take over your loan, they'd only pay you something less than $1,000 because a new loan would earn them more. Your bond lost value even though nothing changed about the promise.

That's the entire inverse relationship in a nutshell. When market rates rise, existing bonds (with their fixed coupons) become less attractive compared to new bonds — so their price falls. When rates fall, existing bonds look great — so buyers bid up their price.

Where the analogy breaks down

The analogy is actually quite accurate! The key nuance: the longer your friend's loan runs (longer maturity), the bigger the price impact of the rate change. A 1-day loan is barely affected by rate changes; a 30-year loan is hugely affected. Also, if your friend promises very frequent payments (high coupon), you get money back quickly, so you're less exposed to future rate changes.

The precise version

Bond price is the present value of all promised cash flows discounted at the required rate of return: P = Σ CF_t/(1+r)^t. When r increases, all discount factors (1+r)^t increase, reducing each PV term and thus total price. The relationship is not linear but convex — price falls at a decreasing rate as rates rise. This is because as rates rise, the present value of distant cash flows shrinks relatively faster than near-term flows, changing the weighting.

2
Duration: What It Really Means
Simple Explanation

Think of a bond's cash flows as weights on a seesaw. Each coupon is a small weight placed at its respective year on the seesaw. The big face-value payment is a heavy weight at maturity. Duration is the balance point — where would you have to put your finger to balance the entire seesaw?

A zero-coupon bond has one heavy weight right at maturity — the balance point is at maturity. That's why duration = maturity for zeros. A high-coupon bond has lots of small weights spread out near the front — so the balance point is much closer to today. That's why high coupons mean shorter duration.

Where the analogy breaks down

The "weights" aren't the raw cash flows but their present values — larger discount rates shrink the far-away weights. This is why higher interest rates also shorten duration (far-away cash flows get discounted more, reducing their relative weight). The seesaw doesn't use raw cash flows; it uses PV-adjusted cash flows.

The precise version

Duration = weighted average time to maturity, where the weights are each cash flow's PV share of the total bond price. Formula: D = [Σ t × PV(CF_t)] / P. Duration doubles as a direct elasticity measure: ΔP/P ≈ −MD × Δr, where Modified Duration MD = D/(1+r). A bond with MD = 5 loses approximately 5% of its value for each 1% rise in rates. This makes duration the single most important risk measure for fixed-income securities.

3
The Gordon Growth Model: Why Growth Rate Matters So Much
Simple Explanation

Imagine a golden goose that lays $100 worth of eggs per year, and you expect this to grow 3% every year forever. If you need a 10% return on your investment, how much would you pay for the goose? The answer is $100/(10%−3%) = $100/7% ≈ $1,429.

Now change the growth rate to 8%. You'd pay $100/(10%−8%) = $100/2% = $5,000. A tiny change in expected growth (3% → 8%) multiplied the goose's value by 3.5x! This is why stock valuations are so sensitive to growth assumptions — small changes in g create massive changes in value because g appears in the denominator.

Where the analogy breaks down

The model assumes growth is constant forever, which is unrealistic. Real companies go through phases: high growth, then maturity, sometimes decline. The model also doesn't capture companies that pay no dividends. Growth rates must always stay below the discount rate, which doesn't apply to individual company growth rates in the short term.

The precise version

The Constant Growth DDM (Gordon Growth Model): P₀ = Div₁/(r_E − g), where Div₁ = next period's dividend, r_E = equity required return, g = perpetual growth rate. Rearranging: r_E = Div₁/P₀ + g = dividend yield + capital gain rate. This model is the cornerstone of equity valuation. For nonconstant growth, use the 3-step process: find PVs of dividends in the high-growth phase, then use the GGM to find terminal value at the end of that phase, and discount everything back.

4
Convexity: Why Duration Is an Optimistic Approximation
Simple Explanation

Imagine you're measuring how steep a hill is at one particular point. Duration is like saying "the hill slopes at 5 degrees right here." If you walk 1 meter in that direction, duration predicts you'll descend a certain amount. But the hill is curved — it gets gentler as you walk. So if you go 10 meters, duration's prediction becomes increasingly wrong.

For bond prices: duration gives you the slope at one rate level. But as rates change, the slope itself changes. The actual bond price curve is always above the duration tangent line — meaning the bond always does better than duration predicts. This "always better than expected" property is what makes convexity valuable.

Where the analogy breaks down

Unlike a physical hill where you might walk any distance, convexity is most relevant for large interest rate movements (1% or more). For small moves (a few basis points), duration alone is fine. Also, some exotic instruments (like callable bonds) can have negative convexity — their price-rate curve bows in the opposite direction — but all standard coupon and zero-coupon bonds have positive convexity.

The precise version

Convexity is the second derivative of the price-rate relationship (duration is the first derivative). The full approximation: ΔP/P ≈ −MD×Δr + ½×Convexity×(Δr)². The second term is always positive (½×Convexity×(Δr)² > 0), meaning the actual price is always above the duration-only prediction. Properties: (1) Convexity is desirable; (2) Greater convexity → more accurate duration; (3) All standard bonds are convex; (4) Zero-coupon bonds have more convexity than coupon bonds of the same maturity.

Tier 1Recall & Basic Calculation
🟢 Q1 · Basic
What are the three types of interest rate measures? State which is ex ante and which is ex post, and define each briefly.
Answer

(1) Required Rate of Return (r) — Ex ante. The rate an investor SHOULD earn given a security's risk. Used to compute fair present value. From Chapter 2's interest rate determinants.
(2) Expected Rate of Return E(r) — Ex ante. The rate an investor EXPECTS to earn by buying at current market price and holding. For bonds, this is the YTM. If markets are efficient, E(r) ≈ r.
(3) Realized Rate of Return (r̄) — Ex post. The rate actually EARNED after the investment period. May differ from required return due to unexpected events.

🟢 Q2 · Basic Calculation
Price a bond: 6% annual coupon, $1,000 face value, 5-year maturity, required return = 8%. Is this a premium, par, or discount bond?
Answer

Annual coupon INT = 0.06 × $1,000 = $60

PV of coupons = 60 × [1−1/(1.08)⁵]/0.08 = 60 × 3.9927 = $239.56

PV of face = 1,000/(1.08)⁵ = 1,000/1.4693 = $680.58

Bond Price = $239.56 + $680.58 = $920.15

This is a DISCOUNT bond — coupon rate (6%) < required rate (8%), so price < $1,000. Calculator: N=5, I/Y=8, PMT=60, FV=1000 → CPT PV = -920.15

🟢 Q3 · Basic
A stock's required return is 12%, and it just paid a $3.00 dividend (Div₀). Dividends grow at a constant 4% per year. What is the stock's fair value today?
Answer

Div₁ = $3.00 × (1.04) = $3.12

P₀ = Div₁ / (r_E − g) = $3.12 / (0.12 − 0.04) = $3.12 / 0.08 = $39.00

Check: Dividend yield = 3.12/39.00 = 8%; Capital gain rate = 4%; Total return = 12% ✓

🟢 Q4 · Basic
State the three features of duration: how does duration change with (a) coupon rate, (b) required rate of return, and (c) maturity?
Answer

(a) Coupon rate ↑ → Duration ↓. Higher coupons return cash sooner, shifting PV weight toward near-term flows.
(b) Required return ↑ → Duration ↓. Higher rates discount distant flows more heavily, reducing their weight in the duration calculation.
(c) Maturity ↑ → Duration ↑, but at a decreasing rate. Duration grows with maturity but approaches an asymptote — each additional year adds less to duration than the previous year.

🟢 Q5 · Basic
A zero-coupon bond has a face value of $1,000, 4-year maturity, and is priced at $792.09. What is its YTM? What is its duration?
Answer

YTM: YTM = (1,000/792.09)^(1/4) − 1 = (1.2625)^(0.25) − 1 = 1.0600 − 1 = 6.0%

Calculator: N=4, PV=-792.09, FV=1000, PMT=0 → CPT I/Y = 6.0%

Duration: For a zero-coupon bond, Duration = Maturity = 4 years. There is only one cash flow (the face value at maturity), so the weighted average time is simply 4 years. Zero-coupon bonds always have the highest duration of any bond with the same maturity.

🟡 Q6 · Intermediate Calculation
A bond has a 9% coupon, $1,000 face value, 4-year maturity (annual payments), and currently sells for $950.76. Find its YTM. Is it a premium or discount bond?
Answer

Annual coupon = 0.09 × $1,000 = $90. We need to find YTM such that:

950.76 = 90/YTM × [1−1/(1+YTM)⁴] + 1,000/(1+YTM)⁴

Calculator: N=4, PV=−950.76, PMT=90, FV=1000 → CPT I/Y = 10.50%

This is a DISCOUNT bond: market price ($950.76) < face value ($1,000), which means YTM (10.5%) > coupon rate (9%). The bond trades at a discount because investors require a higher return than the coupon offers.

🟡 Q7 · Intermediate Calculation
A stock currently pays no dividend but is expected to pay Div₃ = $2.50 starting year 3. After year 3, dividends grow at 6% per year forever. Required return = 11%. What is the stock price today?
Answer

Step 1: PV of Div₃ = 2.50/(1.11)³ = 2.50/1.3676 = $1.828

Step 2: Div₄ = 2.50 × 1.06 = $2.65. Terminal price at end of year 3: P₃ = 2.65/(0.11−0.06) = 2.65/0.05 = $53.00. PV(P₃) = 53.00/(1.11)³ = 53.00/1.3676 = $38.76

Step 3: P₀ = $1.828 + $38.76 = $40.59

🟡 Q8 · Duration Calculation
A bond has a 10% coupon, $1,000 face value, 2-year maturity (annual), and required return = 10% (so it prices at par). Calculate its duration. Then use modified duration to estimate the price change if rates rise by 50 basis points.
Answer

At par (r = 10%): Year 1 CF = $100, Year 2 CF = $1,100. Price = 100/1.10 + 1,100/1.10² = $90.91 + $909.09 = $1,000

Duration:
Year 1: weight = 90.91/1000 = 0.0909; contribution = 1 × 0.0909 = 0.0909
Year 2: weight = 909.09/1000 = 0.9091; contribution = 2 × 0.9091 = 1.8182
D = 0.0909 + 1.8182 = 1.909 years

Modified Duration: MD = 1.909/1.10 = 1.736

Price change for Δr = +0.005 (50 bps):
ΔP/P ≈ −1.736 × 0.005 = −0.00868 = −0.868%
ΔP ≈ −$1,000 × 0.00868 = −$8.68 → New estimated price ≈ $991.32

🟡 Q9 · Intermediate
A stock trades at $50. Next year's expected dividend is $2.50. The stock's required return is 10%. (a) What growth rate is implied? (b) What does this imply about the stock's expected capital gain rate?
Answer

Using r_E = Div₁/P₀ + g:

0.10 = 2.50/50 + g
0.10 = 0.05 + g
g = 5%

(b) The implied capital gain rate = g = 5%. In the Gordon Growth Model, the constant growth rate equals the expected annual price appreciation rate. If the stock is worth $50 today and g = 5%, next year's price should be P₁ = 50 × 1.05 = $52.50.

Verification: Dividend yield (5%) + Capital gain (5%) = 10% required return ✓

🟡 Q10 · Intermediate
Compare two bonds, both with $1,000 face value and 5-year maturity, both priced to yield 8%. Bond A has a 4% coupon; Bond B has a 12% coupon. Without calculating, state which has: (a) higher price, (b) higher duration, (c) more price sensitivity if rates change. Explain each.
Answer

(a) Bond B (12% coupon) has higher price. Higher coupon → higher cash flows → higher PV. Bond A trades at a discount (coupon 4% < yield 8%); Bond B trades at a premium (coupon 12% > yield 8%).

(b) Bond A (4% coupon) has higher duration. Lower coupon → less cash returned early → cash flows are weighted further into the future → longer weighted-average life (duration). The extreme case is a zero-coupon bond: all weight is at year 5, giving duration = 5.

(c) Bond A is more price-sensitive. Higher duration → higher modified duration → larger percentage price change for any given rate change. Bond A's price swings more for the same rate movement. This is why low-coupon bonds are "riskier" in terms of interest rate risk.

🔴 Q11 · Advanced
The Federal Reserve unexpectedly raises interest rates by 1%. Analyze the impact on: (a) a short-term (2-year) high-coupon bond, (b) a long-term (20-year) zero-coupon bond, (c) a stock with very high expected growth. Which loses the most value, and why?
Answer

(a) 2-year high-coupon bond: Short maturity + high coupon → low duration (perhaps 1.5–1.8 years). Modified duration ≈ 1.5. Price falls approximately 1.5%. Relatively modest impact because cash flows are near-term and high in size.

(b) 20-year zero-coupon bond: Duration = maturity = 20 years. Modified duration ≈ 20/1.0x ≈ 19. Price falls approximately 19%. Catastrophic impact — all cash flow is 20 years away and is now discounted much more heavily. This is the most affected of the three by far.

(c) High-growth stock: In the DDM, P₀ = Div₁/(r_E − g). If r_E rises by 1% and g is fixed, the denominator (r_E − g) grows. For a stock with g = 9% and r_E = 10%, the price was 20× Div₁. After rate rise: r_E = 11%, price = Div₁/(0.11−0.09) = 50× Div₁. Wait — the denominator SHRANK from 1% to 2%! Price doubles. But this is a rate decrease example. A rate increase (r_E goes from 10% to 11%) with g = 9%: 10%−9%=1% denominator → 11%−9%=2% denominator. Price falls by 50%! High-growth stocks are enormously sensitive to rate changes because the (r_E − g) spread is tiny.

Most affected overall: The 20-year zero-coupon bond (~19% loss) or high-growth stock (potentially 30–50% loss depending on g), with the short-term high-coupon bond barely moving (~1.5% loss).

🔴 Q12 · Advanced
Explain why convexity makes bonds more valuable, and why an investor should pay MORE (accept a lower yield) for a high-convexity bond versus a low-convexity bond of the same duration. Use the asymmetry of gains vs. losses to support your argument.
Answer

The asymmetry argument: Duration predicts equal price changes for equal rate changes in either direction. But convexity means the actual price curve bends away from this linear prediction — always favorably for the bondholder.

Concretely: If rates rise by 1%, a high-convexity bond loses LESS than duration predicts. If rates fall by 1%, a high-convexity bond GAINS MORE than duration predicts. This asymmetry — "less pain when rates rise, more gain when rates fall" — is pure value for the bondholder.

Why pay more? Imagine two bonds with identical duration (same first-order rate sensitivity). The high-convexity bond outperforms in all interest rate scenarios — it falls less when rates rise and rises more when rates fall. This better performance has value, so rational investors bid its price up (accepting a lower yield) until the additional yield given up equals the value of the convexity advantage.

Practical implication: Portfolio managers actively seek high-convexity bonds during periods of high rate volatility, as the asymmetric payoff is most valuable when large rate moves are likely. Zero-coupon bonds have high convexity for their maturity; callable bonds have negative convexity (the issuer's call option works against the bondholder) — callable bonds must compensate with higher yields.

🔴 Q13 · Advanced
A tech company currently pays no dividend. Analysts expect it to start paying dividends of $5.00 per share in year 5, growing at 20% for 2 more years (years 6–7), then settling at 4% forever. Required return = 12%. Find the current stock price. What does this tell you about how much of a growth stock's value comes from far-future cash flows?
Answer

Step 1: Dividends during high-growth period
Div₅ = $5.00, Div₆ = 5.00 × 1.20 = $6.00, Div₇ = 6.00 × 1.20 = $7.20

PV(Div₅) = 5.00/(1.12)⁵ = 5.00/1.7623 = $2.837
PV(Div₆) = 6.00/(1.12)⁶ = 6.00/1.9738 = $3.040
PV(Div₇) = 7.20/(1.12)⁷ = 7.20/2.2107 = $3.257

Step 2: Terminal value at year 7
Div₈ = 7.20 × 1.04 = $7.488
P₇ = 7.488 / (0.12 − 0.04) = 7.488 / 0.08 = $93.60
PV(P₇) = 93.60/(1.12)⁷ = 93.60/2.2107 = $42.34

Step 3: P₀ = $2.837 + $3.040 + $3.257 + $42.34 = $51.47

Insight: The terminal value PV ($42.34) represents 82% of the total stock price ($51.47)! The first 7 years of dividends contribute only 18% of value. This illustrates why growth stocks are so sensitive to long-run assumptions — the vast majority of value comes from cash flows far in the future, which are highly sensitive to changes in r_E or g.

🔴 Q14 · Advanced
An FI manager holds a bond portfolio with modified duration of 8 years and total value of $50 million. She fears rates will rise by 1.5%. (a) What is the estimated dollar loss using duration? (b) Why will the actual loss be smaller than this estimate? (c) What could she do to reduce the portfolio's interest rate risk?
Answer

(a) Duration-estimated loss:
ΔP/P ≈ −MD × Δr = −8 × 0.015 = −12%
Dollar loss ≈ $50,000,000 × 0.12 = −$6,000,000

(b) Why actual loss is smaller — convexity: Duration provides a linear (tangent line) approximation to the curved price-rate relationship. Because all bonds are convex, the actual price curve lies ABOVE the linear approximation when rates change. A 1.5% rate rise is a large move — convexity's correction is significant. The actual portfolio loss will be less than $6 million because the price-rate curve bends favorably for the holder. To get the precise estimate, add the convexity correction: ΔP/P ≈ −MD×Δr + ½×Convexity×(Δr)², where the convexity term is positive.

(c) Risk reduction strategies:
• Shorten duration — sell long-maturity, low-coupon bonds; buy short-maturity, high-coupon bonds. Reduces price sensitivity.
• Duration matching (immunization) — match asset duration to liability duration so rate changes affect both sides equally.
• Interest rate derivatives — use interest rate swaps (pay fixed, receive floating) or futures to hedge rate exposure without selling bonds.
• Increase convexity — while protecting against rate increases, higher convexity provides a cushion if predictions are wrong.

🔴 Q15 · Advanced
Market efficiency implies that r ≈ E(r). But occasionally, bonds or stocks appear mispriced. Describe (a) what it would mean for a bond's YTM to be greater than its required rate of return, (b) what would happen next in an efficient market, and (c) a real-world reason why mispricing can persist.
Answer

(a) YTM > required rate means the bond is UNDERPRICED. The current market price is below fair value. An investor buying at this price earns a return (YTM) that exceeds what is required to compensate for the bond's risk. This is a bargain — the bond offers more return than necessary for its risk level.

(b) What happens in an efficient market: Sophisticated investors spot the underpricing and buy the bond. This buying pressure drives the market price up, which reduces the YTM (lower price → higher yield, but wait — higher price → lower YTM). As price rises to fair value, YTM falls until YTM = required rate. The mispricing disappears rapidly — in liquid markets, within seconds to minutes. This is the self-correcting mechanism of market efficiency.

(c) Why mispricing can persist in the real world:
• Illiquidity — in thinly traded markets (certain corporate or municipal bonds), there may be no ready buyers even if the bond is underpriced. Trading costs may exceed the profit from exploiting the mispricing.
• Transaction costs — commissions, bid-ask spreads, and taxes can make exploiting small mispricings unprofitable.
• Information asymmetry — some investors may know the bond is risky for reasons not publicly available (e.g., inside knowledge of financial distress), so what appears mispriced is actually fair-priced for informed investors.
• Regulatory constraints — some investors (pension funds, insurance companies) are restricted from buying certain securities regardless of valuation.
• Short-selling restrictions — it's easier to exploit underpricing (buy the asset) than overpricing (requires short-selling, which has friction and risk).

📌 Premium / Discount / Par Rules
  • Coupon < r Discount bond: P < $1,000; YTM > coupon rate
  • Coupon = r Par bond: P = $1,000; YTM = coupon rate
  • Coupon > r Premium bond: P > $1,000; YTM < coupon rate
  • As matures All bonds converge to par ($1,000) at maturity
⚠️ Critical Pitfalls
  • Pitfall 1 Gordon Model: g must be strictly less than r_E
  • Pitfall 2 Semiannual bonds: use r/2 and N×2, not r and N
  • Pitfall 3 Duration ≠ maturity (except for zero-coupon bonds)
  • Pitfall 4 Convexity always benefits the holder (actual > duration estimate)
  • Pitfall 5 Div₁ (not Div₀) goes in numerator of Gordon Model
  • Pitfall 6 YTM assumes all coupons reinvested at YTM rate
📊 Rate Measure Quick Reference
  • r Required rate: fair PV. Ex ante. From Ch. 2 risk premiums.
  • E(r) Expected rate: implied by current price. Ex ante. = YTM for bonds.
  • r̄ Realized rate: what actually happened. Ex post.
  • If r = E(r) Market is efficient. Price = fair value.
  • If r < YTM Bond underpriced → buy signal (in theory).
  • If r > YTM Bond overpriced → sell signal (in theory).
