Section A

Chapter Summary

Core concepts of interest rate determination and valuation.

The Loanable Funds Theory

The loanable funds theory views equilibrium interest rates as the result of the supply of and demand for loanable funds in financial markets. Just as the price of a product is determined by supply and demand, the "price" of money (the interest rate) is determined by the interaction of those who have surplus capital and those who wish to borrow it.

Factor Shift Effect on Interest Rate
Supplier Wealth ↑ Supply Curve Right → Interest Rate ↓
Security Risk ↑ Supply Curve Left ← Interest Rate ↑
Monetary Policy (Expansionary) Supply Curve Right → Interest Rate ↓
Expected Project Returns ↑ Demand Curve Right → Interest Rate ↑
Government Deficit (Borrowing) ↑ Demand Curve Right → Interest Rate ↑ ("Crowding Out")

Nominal vs. Real Interest Rates: The Fisher Effect

Nominal interest rates are the rates actually observed in financial markets. However, investors care about their actual purchasing power, which requires adjusting for inflation. The relationship is defined by the Fisher effect.

The Fisher Effect
(1 + i) = (1 + RFR) × (1 + E(IP))
i = nominal interest rate RFR = real risk-free rate E(IP) = expected inflation premium
Approximation for low rates: i ≈ RFR + E(IP)
📊 Example: Calculating Nominal Rate
1
Given RFR = 2% and expected inflation = 3%.
2
Approximate: 2% + 3% = 5%.
3
Exact: (1.02)(1.03) = 1.0506 → 5.06%.
The nominal rate should be 5.06% to preserve the 2% real return.

Security-Specific Risk Premiums

Beyond the base rate, individual securities have different interest rates due to specific risks and features.

The Fair Interest Rate Equation
ij = IP + RFR + DRPj + LRPj + SCPj + MPj
DRP = Default Risk Premium LRP = Liquidity Risk Premium SCP = Special Provisions (Tax, Callability) MP = Maturity Premium
⚠️
Callability vs. Tax-Exemption

Features that benefit the issuer (like callability) increase the interest rate. Features that benefit the investor (like tax-exempt status on municipal bonds) decrease the interest rate.

Term Structure & Yield Curves

The yield curve plots the relationship between interest rates and time to maturity for similar securities. There are three primary theories explaining its shape:

Unbiased Expectations

Long-term rates are the geometric average of expected future short-term rates. The curve reflects purely market expectations of future rates.

Liquidity Premium

Investors demand a premium for the increased risk of holding longer-term securities. This biases the yield curve upward.

Market Segmentation

Investors stay in specific maturity segments (habitats). Supply and demand in each segment independently determine the rates.

Section C

Feynman Explanations

Breaking down complex theories into simple logic.

🧠

The Fisher Effect

Why inflation and interest rates move together.

Show More ▼

The core idea: Imagine you lend $100 to a friend so they can buy 10 pizzas today. If there is 10% inflation, those 10 pizzas will cost $110 next year. If your friend only pays you back $100, you've lost purchasing power. You'd demand $110 back just to break even, plus a little extra for your trouble (the real return).

In practice: Lenders always add expected inflation to their required "real" return to set the "nominal" rate we see at banks.

The one thing to remember: Nominal rates must rise when inflation expectations rise to keep the real return constant.

⚖️

Loanable Funds Equilibrium

Market forces at work.

Show More ▼

The core idea: Think of interest rates as the "price of renting money." If everyone wants to rent money (high demand) but nobody wants to lend it (low supply), the price of renting goes up. If banks are flooded with cash (high supply) but nobody wants to borrow (low demand), the price falls.

In practice: When the Fed prints money, they increase the "supply," which naturally pushes interest rates down.

The one thing to remember: Interest rates clear the market where the amount of money people want to lend matches the amount others want to borrow.

⏳

Yield Curve Inversion

The economy's warning signal.

Show More ▼

The core idea: Usually, you get paid more for a 30-year loan than a 1-year loan because the future is uncertain. But sometimes, people are so worried about the immediate future that they pay huge rates for short-term cash, while expecting rates to be much lower in the long run when a recession hits.

In practice: An inverted curve means "the market thinks a recession is coming and rates will have to be cut later."

The one thing to remember: Inversion occurs when short-term rates exceed long-term rates, historically predicting a recession.

💡

Time Value of Money (TVM)

Why a dollar today is better than a dollar tomorrow.

Show More ▼

The core idea: If I give you $1 today, you can put it in a bank and have $1.05 next year. If I give you $1 next year, you've missed out on that 5 cents. Therefore, $1 today is "worth more" than $1 later.

In practice: Every financial decision (buying a car, stock, or bond) is just comparing "money today" with "money later."

The one thing to remember: Compounding moves money forward in time; discounting moves money back to today.

Cheat Sheet

Key formulas and common pitfalls.

Key Formulas
  • i = RFR + IP + DRP + LRP + SCP + MP — components
  • (1+i) = (1+RFR)(1+IP) — Fisher Exact
  • FV = PV(1+r)ⁿ — Future Value
  • PV = FV/(1+r)ⁿ — Present Value
  • PV = C/r — Perpetuity
  • t = 72 / r% — Rule of 72
Determinants
  • DRP: Yield difference vs. Treasury
  • LRP: Extra yield for illiquid assets
  • Callability: Benefits issuer → Yield ↑
  • Tax-Exempt: Benefits investor → Yield ↓
Common Pitfalls
  • ⚠️ Confusing nominal vs real rates.
  • ⚠️ Forgetting sign convention on BAII Plus.
  • ⚠️ Applying simple interest when compounding is required.
  • ⚠️ Incorrectly shifting supply/demand curves (Wealth vs Risk).
